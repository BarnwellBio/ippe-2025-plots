Spatial Analysis of Microbial Contamination Sources in Poultry Litter: Influence of Feed, Water, and Environmental Factors on Bacterial Counts

M.A. Peckenschneider1, E.G. Olson1, M.J. McCutchen1, C.E. McGinley2, J.K. Byrnes2, and S.C. Ricke1

1Meat Science and Animal Biologics Discovery, Department of Animal and Dairy Sciences, University of Wisconsin-Madison, Madison, WI, USA, 2Barnwell Bio, Brooklyn, NY, USA

Understanding the spatial distribution of various microbiota in poultry litter is crucial for optimizing biosecurity measures and improving the health and productivity of laying hens. In the current study, we assessed microbial counts in poultry litter with reference to potential sources, including water, feed, and the surrounding environment, across two pens housing laying hens. Litter samples were systematically collected at 28 points following a square grid with 1.5-foot spacing. Each sample was subsequently divided into three technical replicates, with each replicate being dot-plated in duplicate on three different types of media. We quantified aerobic bacteria via Tryptic Soy Agar (TSA), lactic acid bacteria De Man–Rogosa–Sharpe (MRS), and Enterobacteriaceae counts via MacConkey agar's. The samples were diluted, dot plated on appropriate media, and incubated at 37°C for 24 hours. The average microbial counts were log-transformed and analyzed using a linear regression model. As microbial counts did not differ significantly between the two pens (ANOVA, P > 0.05) data from both were combined for subsequent analysis. To assess the relationship between microbial community and distance to various sources, we fitted a linear regression model for each plate type with average microbial count as the response and distance to feed, water, and the external environment as predictors. The results showed a significant linear association for aerobic bacterial counts, where both feed and the interaction between feed and water significantly influenced microbial abundance (P < 0.05). A positive coefficient of 0.78 for feed suggested higher counts at locations farther from the feed source, while a small negative coefficient of -0.09 for the interaction between feed and water was also observed. Lactic acid bacterial counts did not show any significant associations with distance to feed, water, or external environment (P > 0.05). Enterobacteriaceae counts were weakly negatively associated with distance from the external environment (P = 0.05), suggesting a decrease in counts with increasing distance from outside sources. These findings highlight the critical influence of feed, water, and environmental interactions on microbial dynamics in poultry litter, providing valuable insights for enhancing management practices to control contamination and improve poultry health.
